A New Technique to Preserve the Uterus in Patients with Placenta Accreta Spectrum Disorders

Sergey V. Barinov, MD, PhD & Gian Carlo Di Renzo, MD, PhD

Background

  • Placenta accreta spectrum (PAS) disorders: significant maternal morbidity and mortality
  • Often requires hysterectomy
  • Increasing incidence due to rising cesarean delivery rates

Study Objective

Evaluate outcomes of a new uterine-preserving technique (combined approach) vs. standard surgical technique used until 2014

Combined Approach Components

  • Surgical hemostasis
  • Bilateral ligation of descending uterine artery branches
  • Hemostatic external supraplacental stitch
  • Zhukovsky double-balloon tamponade

Study Design

  • Retrospective cohort study
  • 147 patients with PAS disorders
  • Study group (n=95): Combined approach
  • Control group (n=52): Standard technique used until 2014

Patient Demographics

  • Mean age: ~31 years in both groups
  • Majority were multiparous (>70%)
  • 91.2% had history of cesarean delivery
  • 75.5% had at least one comorbidity

PAS Distribution

Study Group:

  • Placenta accreta: 22.1%
  • Placenta increta: 25.3%
  • Placenta percreta: 52.6%

Control Group:

  • Placenta accreta: 46.2%
  • Placenta increta: 42.3%
  • Placenta percreta: 11.5%

Key Results: Hysterectomy

  • Study group: 12.6% underwent hysterectomy
  • Control group: 53.8% underwent hysterectomy
  • 4.5-fold reduction in hysterectomies
  • Odds ratio for hysterectomy: 8.1 (95% CI: 3.6-18.2) in control vs. study group

Key Results: Blood Loss

  • Study group: Median 1300 mL (IQR: 900-2950)
  • Control group: Median 2000 mL (IQR: 1800-4000)
  • 1.5-fold reduction in blood loss volume

Key Results: Blood Transfusion

  • Study group: 8.4% required blood transfusion
  • Control group: 42.3% required blood transfusion
  • 5.1-fold reduction in blood transfusions

Key Results: Hospital Stay

  • Study group: Median 6.5 days (IQR: 5.5-7.0)
  • Control group: Median 10.5 days (IQR: 8.0-13.0)
  • 4-day reduction in hospital stay

Key Results: Bladder Injury

  • Study group: 2.1% experienced bladder injury
  • Control group: 38.5% experienced bladder injury
  • 19-fold reduction in bladder injuries

Combined Approach: Key Components

  1. High transverse uterine incision
  2. Bilateral ligation of descending uterine artery branches
  3. Hemostatic external supraplacental stitch
  4. Zhukovsky double-balloon tamponade
  5. Metroplasty (when necessary)

Zhukovsky Double-Balloon Tamponade

  • Uterine and vaginal balloons used simultaneously
  • Compresses cervical-isthmic collaterals and placental site
  • Provides 10-14 hours of compression
  • Reduces risk of postpartum bleeding and hematoma

Metroplasty Technique

  • Resection of uterine wall with adherent placenta
  • Novel assembly stitch for edge positioning
  • Modified, interrupted, 8-shaped stitch for closure
  • Improves healing and formation of patent uterine scar

Advantages of Combined Approach

  • Significant reduction in hysterectomies
  • Decreased blood loss and transfusion requirements
  • Shorter hospital stays
  • Lower risk of bladder injury
  • Potential for future pregnancies

Limitations of the Study

  • Retrospective design
  • Lack of randomization
  • Definitive PAS diagnosis occurs after delivery

Clinical Implications

  • Potential new standard for managing PAS disorders
  • Increased chances of uterine preservation
  • Reduced maternal morbidity
  • Applicable in various clinical settings

Future Research Directions

  • Prospective, randomized controlled trials
  • Long-term follow-up of patients
  • Evaluation of subsequent pregnancy outcomes
  • Immunohistochemical studies of villus invasion

Conclusion

The combined approach with Zhukovsky double-balloon tamponade significantly reduces hysterectomies, blood loss, transfusions, and hospital stays in patients with PAS disorders, offering a promising new technique for uterine preservation.
